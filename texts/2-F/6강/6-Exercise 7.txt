Until far into the **nineteenth** century, **history** was **seen** as being the **result** of the **actions** of **kings**, **statesmen**, **generals**, and other government **dignitaries**. Readers **trusted** the **historian** to recount the actions of such people; this was the main **indicator** of the historian's **reliability**. It seemed that common sense was all that was needed to be able to give a plausible and convincing account of the actions of kings and statesmen. And common sense is, in Descartes’ well-known view, the most **equally** **distributed** **quality** in the human race. It took a long time before historians began to consider the actions of **ordinary** people and to see history as a **discipline** in its own right.
