A person who always tries to **prevent** harm but never does, is not generally thought of as **morally** good. A morally good person is one who does morally bad actions **significantly** less often than most and does morally good ones significantly more often than most. In **judging** a person not only her actions but also her **intentions** and **motives** are **relevant**. A morally good person must **intend** to do morally good actions and intend to **avoid** morally bad ones. A person who **unintentionally** prevents harm to others and does not harm them simply because things do not **turn out** as she intends is not morally good. Although this kind of situation generally occurs only in slapstick movies, it is worth **mentioning** to avoid the false **impression** that it is the actual **consequences** of a person's actions that count toward her being judged morally good or bad. But actual consequences are important. Of such a person, it may be said that she means well; but, **contrary to** Kant, some results are **necessary** before she is **regarded** as morally good.
